Soft Power

Jul 17th, 2013

Soft power is defined as the ability to “use attraction and persuasion, rather than coercion or payment, to change behaviour.”.

Over the past few years several closely and loosely aligned Non-State Actors have exercised soft power to influence the political landscape. These organisations range from the Hacktivists groups like Anonymous and LulzSec to the whistle-blower organisation Wikileaks, which seeks to reveal secret information from whistle-blowers anonymously and protect them from prosecution.

While LulzSec and Anonymous are traditionally associated with Distributed Denial-Of-Service attacks (DDOS), this isn’t exercising hard power. Because their attacks have no ability to (or have not so far been able to) directly influence the policy of their targets, I would compare them to a “Sit-in” protest rather than a Guerilla attack. Because the attack only serves to bring attention to their cause, I would argue it’s an example of soft power.

Anonymous was able to pull off a raid on Stratfor with “The Global Intelligence Files”, but the exploitation of this raid by leaking the contents of their email archives again displays the use of soft power to achieve their goals.
